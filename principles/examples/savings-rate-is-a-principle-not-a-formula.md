# I save the same percentage of every paycheck regardless of size, and lifestyle creep happens only when the percentage stays whole

> My savings rate is fixed at 30% of net income. When income goes up, savings go up; lifestyle goes up to whatever the remaining 70% allows. The rate is upstream of every spending decision.

## Why it matters

Without a fixed rate, every raise becomes a negotiation about how much "deserves" to go to lifestyle. That negotiation always ends with more lifestyle and the same savings. Fixing the rate makes the negotiation meaningless — the question becomes "what does my remaining 70% look like at this income level?" which is a much healthier question. The number (30%) isn't the principle; the fixed-rate behavior is. The number gets re-set every few years based on where I am in life, but it's never fluid.

## Source / Inspiration

- **External anchor:** the "pay yourself first" principle from a half-dozen personal finance books. Boring but durable.
- **Personal experience:** 2022–2023 I saved a flexible "whatever's left" amount. Realized I was saving 12% on a year I made meaningfully more than a year I'd saved 25% on the same flexible logic. Numbers don't lie; rates do.

## Examples

- **Lived it:** every salary increase since August 2025 has gone 30% to savings before any lifestyle change. I genuinely don't see the savings number anymore — it's automated.
- **Didn't live it:** the bonus I got in March 2024 felt like "free money" so I treated 100% of it as discretionary. Spent ~60% of it within four months on stuff I can barely remember. That's the moment the principle crystallized — the bonus should have followed the same rate.
- **Upcoming:** if the new role I'm looking at comes through, I need to recalibrate the rate before the first paycheck lands. The 30% might be too low at the new income level; I should commit before I see the number.

## Tensions / Where I struggle with this

- The principle says nothing about generosity or giving — those are a separate line item that I haven't disciplined as cleanly. Probably the next principle to articulate.
- 30% feels low compared to what some people I respect are doing (50%+) and high compared to others. I oscillate between "it's not aggressive enough" and "it's more than most people my age" — neither anchor is the right one. The right anchor is "what does this rate make possible in 5 years" and I should think about that more concretely.
- "Lifestyle creep happens only when the percentage stays whole" sounds clean but I've definitely let small things slip — third streaming service, slightly nicer groceries — without adjusting the savings rate up. Worth tracking.
