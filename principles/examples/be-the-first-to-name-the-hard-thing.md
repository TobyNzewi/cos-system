# I name the hard thing first — the broken plan, the missed deadline, the bad assumption — even when I'm the one who broke it

> When something is wrong with a project, I say so before someone else has to. Doesn't matter if I caused it. Naming it first costs less than having it discovered.

## Why it matters

Most people in any room can see when something is broken; almost nobody volunteers to say so. The few who do build a specific kind of trust over time — colleagues stop wondering whether they're getting the truth from me. The cost of being the first to name it is real (about 5 minutes of social discomfort) and the cost of being the second is much higher (people start factoring in that I let it sit). I've watched this dynamic play out across two jobs and three projects; the pattern is stable enough to be a principle.

## Source / Inspiration

- **External anchor:** Dalio's "Radical Transparency" principle (Work Principles, Chapter 3). The shape is the same; the flavor is mine — Dalio is institutional, this is interpersonal.
- **Personal experience:** Q3 2024, I let a client miscommunication slide for two weeks because I didn't want to admit I'd misheard the original ask. When it surfaced, the cleanup was 6x what it would have been. That's the moment this became a rule.

## Examples

- **Lived it:** March 2026, in the kickoff for the new product line, I said "I think the timeline I committed to last week was too aggressive — here's what I think a real one looks like." Nobody else had said it; everyone was relieved when I did. We re-baselined with no drama.
- **Didn't live it:** Late 2024, I sat on the fact that a vendor was missing milestones because I was the one who picked them. By the time it came up in a steering committee, the question was "why didn't you flag this earlier" — which was a fair question and I had no good answer.
- **Upcoming:** the deck I'm shipping next week probably overpromises on what we can demo; I need to call that out in the pre-read rather than letting it land in the room.

## Tensions / Where I struggle with this

- It makes me look bad in the moment. The instinct to wait one more day to see if it fixes itself is *strong*.
- It can backfire when the room isn't ready for it — sometimes naming the hard thing makes me the problem in the eyes of someone whose project I'm flagging. Reading the room matters; the principle is "first to name," not "loudest to name."
- I don't extend this consistently to my personal life — I'll name a broken project at work but not a broken assumption in a relationship. That's a gap.
