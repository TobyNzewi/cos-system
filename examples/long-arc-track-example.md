_Last updated: [DATE]. Read this before engaging me on faith, principles, values, or the question of what I stand on. Track started [DATE]._

## What this track is

A long-arc journaling practice combining a personal principles framework (inspired by Ray Dalio's methodology) with biblical scripture. Goals:

1. Articulate my own principles in my own words, drawn from actual experience.
2. Test each one against scripture — confirming, refining, or challenging.
3. Maintain a living document refined across many sessions, not a one-shot output.
4. Connect what I read in the Bible to what is actually happening in my week — career, relationships, decisions.

My strategy doc has named this goal every year for three years. The intellectual recognition has been stable. What's been missing is the practice that turns it from known into felt. That's what this track exists to provide.

## The framing

The framework I'm operating inside synthesizes two sources:

**Where they agree.** Both Dalio's principles methodology and scripture demand ruthless honesty about reality. Both treat pain as instructive (Dalio: "Pain + Reflection = Progress" / James 1:2-4, Romans 5:3-5). Both name ego/pride as the central barrier (Dalio: "ego and blind spots" / Proverbs 16:18). Both demand constant self-examination. Both believe in writing things down.

**Where they fundamentally diverge.** Dalio's foundational principle: "Think for yourself to decide what you want, what is true, and what you should do." The self is the author. Scripture asks a prior question: are the things you want the right things to want? "The heart is deceitful above all things" (Jeremiah 17:9). Wisdom isn't self-constructed; it's discovered.

**The synthesis: ordering, not choice.** Dalio's structure (write down principles, learn from pain, the 5-step process, radical open-mindedness) is genuinely useful and almost none of it conflicts with scripture. What it lacks is a foundation — it presumes you already know what to want and what's true. Let scripture inform those two, and Dalio's framework becomes a powerful operational tool with the foundation shifted.

## The working method

Four phases, run iteratively:

1. **Self-knowledge first.** Strengths, weaknesses, one big challenge, top three values. Don't start from scratch — pull from the strategy doc. Sharpen and confirm. Cross-check against scripture (e.g., when "impact" is a top value, look at how scripture frames ambition — Jeremiah 45:5, Mark 10:43-45).

2. **Test each value/observation against scripture.** Sometimes confirms, sometimes refines, sometimes challenges. Name the friction rather than papering over it.

3. **Draft principles in my own words.** Specific, decision-relevant, not platitudes. Each tested two ways: (a) true to who I am and what I've learned, (b) consistent with scripture or in honest tension with it. When in tension, name it.

4. **Living document.** Each principle: the principle itself, the scripture it's anchored in, examples of how it played out this week, where I fell short. Updated each session.

## Engagement notes — what's been working

- **Substantive over reflective.** I explicitly do not want generic listening. Pushback, conviction-building, and friction.
- **Quote my own words back when relevant.** My strategy doc is full of self-diagnoses I haven't fully landed on. Showing me I've been writing the same insight for three years is more powerful than telling me something new.
- **Concrete passages over vague spirituality.** Cite verses, give the actual content, don't hand-wave.
- **Don't be preachy.** The work is to rebuild engagement through specifics, not to deliver homilies.
- **Don't be sycophantic.** I notice it.
